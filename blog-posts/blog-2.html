My Folder Structure

The reason for my unconventional folder structure in my website stems from the experimental nature of its development. Initially, as I worked on creating the website, I engaged in a process of trial and error, exploring different layouts and arrangements. However, as I began organising my pages into folders, I encountered difficulties with the navigation between the various pages. Despite attempts to rectify the issue, the flow of the website was disrupted. Consequently, I reverted back to the original folder structure to ensure the website's functionality and navigation. Currently, the website comprises a single "Images" folder alongside ten individual pages, including "about," "blog-1" through "blog-4," "blogs," "designs," "essays," "index," and "portfolio." This structure, although unconventional, maintains the website's operational integrity while providing access to its content.

My wireframe ideas

A good wireframe serves as the blueprint for a website, outlining its structure, layout, and functionality in a simplified manner. It accomplishes several key objectives, including providing a clear visualisation of the site's content hierarchy, navigation flow, and user interaction pathways. By focusing on the fundamental elements without distractions such as colors or images, a wireframe allows designers and stakeholders to concentrate on the core features and user experience. This approach fosters efficient communication, enabling early feedback and iteration to refine the design before moving into the development phase. Moreover, a website with ample negative space and predominantly image-based content offers a visually engaging and intuitive user experience.

My strategy of using negative space allows for better readability, clearer focus on key elements, and enhanced user comprehension. My images, when used effectively, can convey information quickly and evoke emotions, enriching the overall user experience. By prioritising such design principles, I aim to create a website that not only captivates users visually but also guides them seamlessly through content and interactions, ultimately enhancing their satisfaction and engagement.


Reference websites

Visit the Museo Del Prado Website

The Museo del Prado's website showcases some of Spain's best fine art, with paintings spanning the entire screen in enough detail to reveal the textures of the brush strokes. It's as if you're right up against the artwork while watching it. Another feature I like is how each background image adjusts to the shape of the viewport.


Visit the Frans Hals Museum Website

The Frans Hals Museum exhibits both modern and 16th and 17th century art, including works by Frans Hals, a portrait artist.Its website is possibly my favorite, with a perfect balance of animation, usability, color, and subtle nuances on each page. I adore the elaborate scroll effects, as well as the sticky sidebar that follows you as you scroll, ensuring that you always have access to the most crucial links.


Visit the Rijksmuseum Website

The Rijksmuseum in Amsterdam's website does not waste time in getting visitors up close and personal with the art, allowing them to see the finer aspects of the paintings on display. They also provide a number of high quality video tours to help visitors better grasp the museum's offerings.
