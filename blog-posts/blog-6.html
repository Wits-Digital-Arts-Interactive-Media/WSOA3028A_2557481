Digital Divide Widens by Maria Tataki & Dimitris Glynos

The COVID-19 pandemic has magnified the existing disparities in access to digital resources, amplifying the digital divide worldwide. With the sudden transition to online education and teleworking, it has become evident that these opportunities are predominantly accessible to a privileged segment of society. This unequal distribution of access has left a significant portion of students behind, with approximately 50% lacking access to computers and 40% devoid of internet connectivity at home. The repercussions are particularly severe in low-income regions like Sub-Saharan Africa, where a staggering 82% of students are unable to access online resources. Consequently, marginalized groups, including vulnerable students and less privileged workers, face heightened challenges in maintaining educational and professional continuity.

Moreover, specific demographic groups encounter distinct obstacles in accessing e-learning, further exacerbating the digital divide. Girls, in particular, are disproportionately affected, as they often have limited access to technology compared to their male counterparts. Additionally, students from low-income countries face significant barriers due to resource constraints and inadequate digital infrastructure. This widening gap not only deepens existing inequalities but also poses long-term consequences for educational attainment and socio-economic mobility.

In response to these challenges, short-term solutions have been implemented to address the immediate digital gap. Initiatives such as providing laptops to students, distributing free SIM cards for internet access, and offering data usage schemes aim to alleviate the immediate barriers to online learning. However, there is also a concerted effort to develop sustainable, long-term strategies to bridge the digital divide beyond the pandemic. These strategies prioritize addressing underlying issues such as internet affordability and availability, with the goal of ensuring equitable access to digital infrastructure for all individuals. By fostering a more inclusive digital environment, these efforts aim to uphold the fundamental right to education and empower individuals to thrive in an increasingly digital world.


Internet Access Is A Fundamental Right by Spyros Kasapis

Internet access is increasingly recognized as a fundamental right due to the emergence of digital inequality, which deprives nearly half of the global population of access to essential digital networks. This lack of access perpetuates economic disparities by limiting individuals' opportunities in education, healthcare, and the job market. From a human rights perspective, internet access is deemed crucial for equal participation in the digital economy and society, akin to other fundamental rights like education and healthcare. The COVID-19 pandemic has accentuated the importance of internet access as a fundamental right, particularly in light of increased reliance on digital connectivity for remote work, online education, telemedicine, and virtual social interactions.

The pandemic has exacerbated the digital divide by highlighting disparities in access to the internet, especially concerning remote work, online commerce, education, healthcare, and social interactions. Individuals without internet access are disadvantaged in participating in the digital economy, accessing educational resources, receiving healthcare information, and maintaining social connections during periods of lockdown. International organizations such as the United Nations have played a pivotal role in recognizing internet access as a human right through various initiatives and declarations. They advocate for universal internet access, monitor global digital rights, and provide recommendations to address digital inequalities and promote digital inclusion. Through resolutions and global advocacy efforts, the UN emphasizes the importance of internet access in upholding fundamental human rights, fostering human development, and bridging digital divides worldwide.

Efforts to address the digital divide include short-term solutions such as lending laptops to students, providing free SIM cards, and offering free data usage for mobile users. These initiatives aim to bridge the immediate digital gap and enable individuals to access educational resources online. In the long term, there is a focus on sustaining efforts to provide access to digital infrastructure beyond the pandemic response. This involves addressing underlying issues like affordability and availability of internet access. By implementing long-term strategies, it is hoped that the digital transition will persist and ensure that all individuals have the opportunity to access educational resources regardless of their circumstances. Through these efforts, the goal is to create a more equitable digital landscape where internet access is accessible to all, promoting equal opportunities and social inclusion.
